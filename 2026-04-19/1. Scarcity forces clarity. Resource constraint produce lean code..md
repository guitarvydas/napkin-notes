## Scarcity of hardware
In the early days of programming, e.g. the mid 20th century, "computer" hardware was scarce and very expensive.

CPUs cost a lot of money. So we spent human resources (brain power, human thinking time) on coming up with ways to time-share CPUs, allowing one CPU to run many programs. This actually slowed down programs, since they each got only a small slice of CPU time, but CPUs ran so fast that, in human terms, they all appeared to be running simultaneously in parallel. This is not actually the case, it only appears that way to human observers.

Memory cost a lot of money back then, so we spent human resources on conserving memory and recycling it. We spent lots of time developing garbage collection algorithms. We spent hardware resources on doing incremental garbage collection using stack frames and calling conventions. "Local variables" are automagically gc'ed when the encompassing subroutine terminates. These optimizations, of course, came with baggage in the form of gotchas, namely the concept of left-over information that could be used in unintended ways unless explicitly destroyed ("undefined") by the hardware. This cost more hardware cycles and made balancing the trade-offs harder and harder.

The seed observation is correct but incomplete as stated, because it smuggles in an assumption: that the relevant resource stays the same over time. The document's real contribution is identifying that the resource _changed_, and that our tools and paradigms didn't change with it.

**The scarcity flip**

When hardware was the scarce resource, spending human ingenuity to conserve it was rational. Time-sharing, garbage collection, calling conventions, AOT compilation — these were good trades. Human time was cheap relative to CPU cycles and memory. But the decisions made under that constraint didn't come with expiry dates. They calcified into orthodoxy. Lexical scoping, AOT bias, the suppression of JIT approaches like Forth's — these were efficiency decisions that got promoted to correctness decisions, and then to aesthetic ones. By the time hardware became cheap, the people making language decisions had internalized the old constraints as permanent truths.

Now the scarce resource is human comprehension, and we are largely still spending it to conserve hardware that costs almost nothing.

**The referential transparency bargain is the clearest example**

The one-in-one-out function is a comprehension tool — it makes local reasoning possible by eliminating time and ordering from the picture. That's valuable. But the cost is that time and ordering don't disappear from the actual problem — they get pushed to the boundaries, where they accumulate as accidental complexity. Callback hell and ethernet stack design are both examples of temporal complexity that was excluded from the core notation and then had to be managed outside it, with much greater difficulty than if the notation had admitted it honestly from the start.

ICs knew this all along. An IC has always had multiple inputs, multiple outputs, enable pins, timing constraints, and propagation delays. The hardware engineers never had the luxury of pretending time didn't exist. Software engineers bought themselves that luxury at the cost of a very large deferred debt.

**The AST/AOT point is underappreciated**

The move to AST-based compilers feels like it's preserving brain power — you parse once, build a rich representation, and then reason over it. But it imposes a hidden architectural cost: everything must be known before anything can be executed. This couples stages together in ways that resist the kind of dynamic, staged, message-passing architectures that would actually serve 21st century problems better. Forth's approach — parse a word, compile or execute it immediately, move on — is more naturally suited to streaming, incremental, distributed computation. It lost not because it was wrong but because it was cache-unfriendly on 1980s hardware and didn't fit the AOT compiler pipeline that academia had already standardized around.

**The VISA bill framing is worth keeping**

No single decision produced bloat. Each trade-off made local sense under the constraints of its moment. But the accumulation — GC overhead, calling convention preamble, exception handling bolted on, async wrappers around sync primitives, type system machinery to manage what disciplined design would have prevented — adds up to systems that spend most of their complexity budget on accidental problems. The constraint that produced each line item is gone. The charge remains.

**The implied prescription**

If brain power is now the scarce resource, the right optimization target is _semantic density_ — how much design intent can be expressed per unit of cognitive load. Mathematical notation achieves this for its domain through extreme abbreviation and convention. Your little-languages approach achieves it by matching notation to problem shape. Both are responses to the same scarcity. The difference from 20th century optimization is that you're no longer trying to conserve memory or cycles — you're trying to conserve the programmer's ability to hold the system in their head at the right level of abstraction.
