# Turing Completeness

Why do we strive for Turing Completeness in programming languages?

How does it relate to 21st century problem domains, like internet, robotics, IoT, long-distance distributed computers, asynchronous parallelism, etc.?

---

**Your question:** _Why do we strive for Turing Completeness in programming languages? How does it relate to 21st century problem domains?_

---

**Why we strove for it historically**

Turing completeness was a theoretical achievement before it was an engineering goal. Turing's original insight was mathematical — defining the boundary of what is mechanically computable at all. When early language designers aimed for Turing completeness, they were solving a legitimate problem: proving that a high-level notation could express anything a machine could do, so that nothing was left on the table. In the context of the 1950s and 1960s, when the question was whether high-level languages were even viable, this was a reasonable ambition.

The problem is that the goal calcified. Turing completeness became a badge of seriousness, a threshold below which a language wasn't considered a "real" language. This is a category error — Turing completeness is a property of computational expressiveness, not of usefulness or fitness for purpose. A language that can simulate a Turing machine is not thereby good at expressing network protocols, or robot behavior, or distributed state machines.

**What Turing completeness actually guarantees**

Turing completeness guarantees only one thing: that a language can compute anything that is computable, given enough time and memory. It says nothing about whether the language can express the computation _clearly_, _safely_, _concurrently_, or _at the right level of abstraction_. It is a minimum bar for expressive power, not a measure of expressive quality. Striving for it in every language is like insisting every tool be capable of driving a nail — technically achievable with almost anything, but not the point.

**The 21st century mismatch**

The problem domains you list share a structural property: they are inherently distributed, asynchronous, spatially separated, and time-dependent. They involve multiple independent agents that communicate by passing information across physical boundaries, with no shared clock, no shared memory, and no guarantee of ordering. This is the opposite of what Turing completeness, as typically implemented, is good at.

A Turing machine is a single locus of control moving sequentially through a tape. The internet is millions of independent loci of control communicating asynchronously across unreliable channels. Robotics involves sensors and actuators operating in continuous time with physical constraints. IoT involves deeply constrained devices reacting to environmental events in parallel. None of these fit the single-threaded, sequential, time-free model that Turing completeness was designed to characterize.

Striving for Turing completeness in languages for these domains is not wrong exactly — it is simply aimed at the wrong target. The hard problems in distributed systems are not about expressive completeness. They are about ordering guarantees, failure modes, partial availability, message delivery semantics, and temporal behavior. A Turing-complete language that handles none of these well is less useful for the domain than a non-Turing-complete notation that handles all of them clearly.

**The little languages implication**

If you accept that different problem aspects need different notations, Turing completeness becomes a workflow-level property rather than a per-language requirement. The workflow as a whole needs to be able to express anything the problem requires. Individual languages within the workflow can be deliberately incomplete — and their incompleteness is a feature, not a deficiency, because it constrains what can be expressed and therefore what can go wrong. A state machine notation that cannot express arbitrary computation cannot accidentally introduce arbitrary computation where only state transitions belong. A dataflow notation that cannot express shared mutable state cannot accidentally introduce shared mutable state where only message passing belongs.

This reframes the question entirely. Instead of asking "is this language Turing complete?" the useful question becomes "is this language complete for its intended subdomain?" — and completeness for a subdomain is a much tighter, more verifiable, and more useful property than general Turing completeness.

**The deeper irony**

The 21st century problem domains you list are all, at bottom, better modeled by things we already understand and that predate the Turing completeness obsession: communicating automata, Petri nets, process calculi, dataflow graphs. These models were developed precisely to reason about concurrent, asynchronous, distributed computation. They are in some cases less than Turing complete by design, because their designers understood that restricting expressiveness is how you make systems analyzable and correct. The field largely ignored them in favor of shoehorning everything into the sequential Turing-complete model, and is now spending enormous effort — in the form of async runtimes, actor frameworks, distributed transaction systems — rebuilding those older models on top of the wrong foundation.
