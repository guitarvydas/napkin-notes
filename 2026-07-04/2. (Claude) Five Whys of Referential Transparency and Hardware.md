Q: What about referential transparency? Isn't that a way to compose functions, like LEGO® blocks? 
A: Yes — same input, same output, anywhere.
Q: Why does that matter?
A: You can swap pieces without breaking the whole.
Q: Why does swapping matter?
A: It lets you reason locally, not globally.
Q: Why is local reasoning valuable? 
A: It scales — no need to track hidden state.
Q: Why does hidden state break things? 
A: It makes output depend on context, not just input — pieces stop being interchangeable.
Q: Why does this matter in hardware?
A: Circuits are built from composable, side-effect-free blocks too.
Q: How so?
A: A logic gate's output depends only on its current inputs.
Q: Why is that useful?
A: You can verify a block in isolation, then trust it in any circuit.
Q: What happens without that property?
A: Feedback loops and shared state make timing and correctness hard to verify.
Q: What's the hardware analog of a side effect?
A: State that persists across clock cycles — like a latch or memory cell.
