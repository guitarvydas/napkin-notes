**Fact-check: PowerShell and structured pipes** Yes, this is correct. PowerShell pipes .NET objects between cmdlets, not raw byte streams. The receiving cmdlet can directly access named properties of the incoming object without parsing. This is a genuine architectural difference from UNIX pipes and was a deliberate design choice by Jeffrey Snover.

**On the broader argument**

You're essentially mapping the seam problem onto a spectrum:

|Approach|Pro|Con|
|---|---|---|
|Binary blobs (UNIX pipes)|Universal, zero schema|Receiver must know format implicitly|
|Structured objects (PowerShell)|No parsing, type-safe|Tight coupling, AOT contract required|
|Strings + parsing (PEG/OhmJS)|Flexible, distributed-friendly|Parsing cost, grammar versioning|
|Triple factbases|Normalized, machine-friendly, extensible|Low human readability|
|LLM-mediated extraction|Maximally flexible, tolerant of shape variance|Non-deterministic, latency, trust|

The interesting insight in your framing is that these aren't mutually exclusive — they sit at different points on a **coupling vs. flexibility** axis, and a well-designed workflow might use different seam types at different boundaries depending on how tightly coupled those components _should_ be.

**On the triple/factbase idea**

This is undersold in mainstream practice. Triples are:

- Naturally additive (you can extend a factbase without breaking existing consumers)
- Already the foundation of RDF and Datalog, which have mature query semantics
- A good fit for the "receiver extracts what it wants" model — consumers pattern-match against the facts they care about and ignore the rest

The human-readability objection is real but arguably misplaced — if the factbase is the _wire format_ between little languages, human readability is less important than machine regularity. Each little language's _own_ notation handles human readability at the edges.

**The LLM angle is genuinely novel here**

Traditionally, the sender-receiver contract is a hard engineering problem — both sides must agree on schema, and schema drift breaks things. LLMs soften this considerably. A receiver that uses an LLM to extract what it needs from a loosely-shaped input blob is essentially doing **intent-based parsing** rather than **schema-based parsing**. This tolerates sender evolution gracefully. The tradeoffs (non-determinism, cost, latency) are real but shrinking. This could be a meaningful architectural primitive for loosely coupled little-language workflows in a way that simply wasn't practical five years ago.

Having read the article, this is a rich reference. A few observations on how it connects to what we've been discussing:

**Werner's sharpest contribution: "semantic late binding"**

Traditional late binding defers _which function to call_. Werner pushes this further — the receiver decides what the message _means_ at runtime. He frames this as deferring everything to runtime, not just which function to call, but what the message even means — letting the receiver decide. This is precisely the mechanism that makes LLM-mediated seams between little languages tractable. The sender doesn't need to know the receiver's schema because meaning is negotiated at the boundary, not agreed upon in advance.

**The Alan Kay connection is apt but also interesting in its tension**

Kay's OOPSLA '97 vision was about objects as autonomous little computers communicating by messages — which maps cleanly onto your little-languages idea. But Kay was famously skeptical of stringly-typed, loosely-defined interfaces. Werner's natural-language messages would likely have made Kay uncomfortable precisely _because_ they're semantically ambiguous. Yet Werner's counter-argument is essentially: LLMs give us the runtime Smalltalk always wanted, because they can interpret — take a message and decide what it means, describe their own capabilities in natural language, and negotiate interfaces on the fly. The ambiguity isn't a bug; it's what enables the biological-cell-like autonomy Kay wanted.

**Where it intersects your triple/factbase idea**

Werner's prompt objects and your factbase-as-wire-format are complementary rather than competing. A factbase of triples is a disciplined, machine-regular form of the "natural language message" — less expressive but more deterministic. You could imagine a spectrum: raw prose messages (Werner) → lightly structured triples → fully schematized objects (PowerShell), with LLMs operating comfortably anywhere on that spectrum as the interpreting layer.

**The Sapir-Whorf observation is worth dwelling on**

Werner notes that "agent" primes you toward autonomy, tasks, and guardrails, while "prompt object" primes you toward composition, interfaces, and message protocols. This mirrors your own point about notation shaping thought — the same principle that makes DPLs better than TPLs for control flow applies to what we _call_ our computational units. The word "agent" has arguably pushed the field toward the wrong set of questions.
