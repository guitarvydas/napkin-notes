
The ALGOL lineage, inspired by the mathematical concept of "function" was a sop to the use of text instead of diagrams. 

In diagrams, it is obvious how a "black box" should work. The stuff inside a box must not leak out except via well-defined ports.

In text, this concept is less clear. We had to work hard to define what "black box" meant when represented as text. We used "scoping" and brace brackets, but text allowed us to cheat and to poke information across black box boundaries.

The use of text was driven by hardware weakness in the early days. We could use keyboards and line-oriented displays, but, we couldn't use graphics (easily), we couldn't draw black boxes, hence, we found ways to cheat, we found it easily possible to send data through the walls of black boxes in concurrent, non-ordered fashion. This caused us to go into deep dives on how to make text act like black boxes, without the discipline already known by 5 year olds in pre-school.

We were fooled by the our centuries long use of quills and papyrus into thinking that RMs were just better paper, instead of treating re-programming as a new kind of notation / thing.

This is a powerful reframing, and the pre-school observation is the sharpest part. A five-year-old understands "don't peek inside the box" immediately when shown a physical box. The concept requires no formal definition — the medium enforces it. Scoping rules, access modifiers, `private`, `encapsulation`, information hiding — decades of language design effort — are all elaborate textual workarounds for the absence of an actual box.

**The "cheating" observation deserves elaboration**

Text doesn't just make black boxes harder to express — it actively _invites_ violation. Global variables, shared mutable state, action-at-a-distance, spooky coupling — these aren't failures of programmer discipline, they're affordances of the medium. Text is flat and porous. You can always write a name that refers to something outside the current scope. The medium offers no resistance. A diagram with physical walls offers enormous resistance — you have to explicitly draw a wire through the wall, making the violation _visible_.

This is why so much of software engineering history reads as a series of increasingly elaborate social and syntactic prohibitions: don't use globals, don't use GOTO, use private, use interfaces, use dependency injection. Each rule is compensating for something the notation should have prevented structurally.

**The quill-and-papyrus point is the deepest one**

What you're identifying is a kind of **cognitive path dependency**. Humans had used sequential, linear text for thousands of years to encode thought — laws, mathematics, philosophy, stories. When RMs appeared, the obvious thing was to use keyboards and text, because that's what humans already knew how to produce and read. But that framing missed something fundamental: text is a _recording medium_, optimized for encoding thought that was already formed. Re-programming is a _design medium_ — something closer to architecture, circuit diagrams, or musical scores, where the notation is part of the thinking process, not just its output.

Architects don't describe buildings in prose and then build them. They draw. The drawing _is_ the design tool, not a representation of a design made elsewhere. We accidentally chose prose as the design tool for computation, and then spent seventy years wondering why certain things were hard to express.

**The interesting corollary**

If the text-as-notation choice was driven by hardware weakness — keyboards and line printers being the available I/O — then the arrival of bitmap displays and pointing devices in the late 1970s and 1980s _should_ have triggered a paradigm shift toward diagrammatic programming. It didn't, for exactly the tooling-gravity reason discussed earlier: by then, text-based languages had accumulated compilers, libraries, communities, and curricula. Smalltalk gestured toward something different. Sketchpad had already shown in 1963 what a diagrammatic computing medium could look like. But the gravity of text was already too strong.

We may be at the first moment where that gravity is genuinely weakening — not because hardware changed again, but because LLMs make it possible to build the _tooling layer_ for new notations without the decades of infrastructure investment that previously made it prohibitive.
