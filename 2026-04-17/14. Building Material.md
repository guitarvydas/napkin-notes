The conflation point is the key move here, and it has a precise form: we took the properties of the _building material_ and promoted them to the status of _universal design principles_. This is like insisting that all architecture must express the properties of concrete — that curves are suspect because concrete wants to be rectangular, that transparency is unnatural because concrete is opaque.

**The "accidental complexity" diagnosis is exactly right**

Fred Brooks distinguished accidental complexity from essential complexity. Essential complexity is inherent in the problem. Accidental complexity is introduced by the tools. Most of what makes concurrent, distributed, asynchronous systems hard to write in functional/sequential languages is _accidental_ — it's the friction of forcing a non-sequential problem into a sequential notation. Promises, async/await, monads, continuations, reactive streams — these are all accidental complexity. They're the notation straining against the problem shape.

**The "little engines" framing is important**

What you're pointing at is a two-level architecture that almost nobody consciously adopts:

- **Level 1:** CPU chips + sequential code as raw building material — value-neutral, no paradigm implied
- **Level 2:** Engines built _from_ that material that provide genuine scaffolding for the target paradigm — whether that's async message-passing, statecharts, dataflow, constraint systems, or something else

What we actually do is collapse these two levels. We use the building material directly as the design medium, which means the building material's properties leak upward and constrain the design. It's the equivalent of writing your architecture directly in machine code — technically possible, practically catastrophic for anything above a certain complexity threshold.

**The paradigm monopoly has a historical explanation but not a justification**

The functional/sequential paradigm won not because it was the best fit for the most important problems, but because:

1. It was the easiest to compile efficiently on early hardware
2. It mapped onto mathematical notation that educated people already knew
3. It accumulated tooling, curricula, and communities first

None of these are arguments that it's the right paradigm for distributed systems, robotics, UI, networking, or any of the domains that now constitute most of what software actually does. They're arguments that it won a historical race whose finishing line was defined by 1970s hardware constraints.

**The deepest version of your point**

"Programming" as currently practiced isn't even a good abstraction over CPUs — it's a _particular ideological reading_ of what CPUs make possible, filtered through mathematical prejudice and hardware limitations that no longer exist. CPUs support mutation, jumps, parallelism, interrupts, and direct memory manipulation. The functional/sequential paradigm systematically suppresses most of these capabilities in the name of "correctness" and "reasoning," and then spends enormous effort re-introducing them (as monads, as async runtimes, as garbage collectors) in forms that fit the paradigm's aesthetic preferences.

The result is, as you say, building material used to construct a single, narrow scaffold — and then mistaking that scaffold for the only possible shape of computation.
