Dynamic binding followed a similar arc. Early Lisp used dynamic binding — variables were resolved at runtime, based on the environment at the moment of the call. This gave the system flexibility: behavior could change depending on context, without the caller needing to know in advance. That flexibility was later judged to be a liability. It made programs harder to analyze at compile time, because the compiler could not know which binding a variable would resolve to until the program actually ran. The fix was static scoping: variables resolved at compile time, based on where they appeared in the source, not on what was happening at runtime. Static scoping made compile-time analysis tractable. Type checkers, optimizers, and formal verification tools all depend on it. The trade seemed permanent. Then we introduced JIT compilation — just-in-time, meaning the compiler runs during execution, making decisions based on what the program is actually doing at that moment. That is runtime dynamism. That is, in structural terms, what dynamic binding provided. We decided runtime flexibility was a problem, built decades of tooling on the assumption of its absence, and then built more tooling to bring it back in a different form. Lisp 1.5 had runtime dynamism in 1962. We removed it, spent years on the consequences of removing it, and recovered it after a long detour. The net position is approximately where we started, at considerably greater expense.

---

The additions: the explanation of _why_ dynamic binding was removed (compile-time analysis became tractable with static scoping, and that's what the whole tooling stack depends on), and the explicit statement that JIT is structurally the same thing as what was removed.
