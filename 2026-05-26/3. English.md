fact check: do the words "concurrency" and "parallelism" mean the same in English?

Not quite — and it matters for the rhetorical setup.

In plain English, _concurrent_ means happening at overlapping times (possibly interleaved, possibly not). _Parallel_ carries the stronger sense of happening simultaneously _and_ independently, side by side without intersection — like parallel lines that never meet.

Legal usage makes the distinction clear: "concurrent sentences" are served during the same time period, but the activities (the sentences) aren't truly simultaneous and independent in the way "parallel" implies.

So the opening claim — "in English they mean essentially the same thing" — is approximately true in casual speech, but not as tight as the article asserts. A pedantic reader can push back before you even get to the CS distinction.

A more defensible opener would be something like: _In casual English, the words are often used interchangeably. In computer science, the distinction is precise and consequential._ That sets up the same contrast without overstating the English-language equivalence.
