
Every electronic component is a physical object that must obey the laws of physics. The "semantics" of electronic components consists of stimulus (inputs as a function of time) vs response (outputs as a function of time). Physical electronic components are completely isolated from one another and completely asynchronous - given an input, they immediately proceed creating output - at their own (top) speed regardless of how other components in the circuit react to their inputs.

In contrast, written mathematical notation is not fundamentally a function of time. Functions do not need to obey the laws of physics, when those functions are written down on paper. "Referential transparency" can happen instantaneously on paper - it _could_ occur "faster than the speed of light". When one maps this kind of notation onto physical devices, though, the notation falters - suddenly, the laws of physics begin to matter, time begins to matter. One can continue to ignore these realities and simply use the notation as a spherical cow. This notation is quite useful for the small subset of problems that can be solved that way, i.e. without regard for the laws of physics, time, speed of light, etc. For problems that fall outside of such a sweet spot, we need to kludge the notation and/or suffer unexpected gotchas (callback hell, Mars Pathfinder fiasco, all sorts of self-inflicted complexity (promises, etc.), etc., etc.)

Observation shows that the electronics schematic approach worked and handled Moore's Law gracefully without a lot of hand-wringing. Observation shows that the software (synchronous / sequential) approach never had an equivalent of Moore's Law and continuously runs aground on unexpected gotchas. Observation shows that human speech is sequential and synchronous, whereas just about everything else in humanity and its reality is asynchronous. This suggests, at least to me, that a synchronous / sequential approach should only be a _subset_ of a more general approach based on asynchronicity and non-sequentiality. Things like "par" and thread libraries are vain attempts at putting asynchronicity back in when it was previously discarded - at best a big noop, but, more likely, simply impossible.

That is what I see in electronic schematic notation. Parts on a schematic are naturally referentially transparent and have multiple ports and are expected to obey the laws of physics (i.e. inputs and outputs are functions of time, not just instantaneous blobs of data that can be further deconstructed into various "types"). 

Maybe functional notation and "computation" work well on paper, but when mapped onto real, law abiding hardware, problems arise.

On the surface, it sounds like asynchronicity _should_ be harder to deal with than synchronicity, but, unbiased observation does not support this view. Thread libraries, "par", "async", promises, etc., etc. are clumsy ways to express the simple concepts of asynchronicity and parallelism. Humanity has dealt with asynchronicity for 1,000s of years (longer than the existence of speech and text) and humanity has developed protocols to manage this kind of thing ("handshaking", "starting the meeting on time", "waiting for the main speaker to arrive", etc. (These have been re-discovered and given different names in software, e.g. "network protocol", "permissionless blockchain", "permissioned blockchain", etc.)).

I learned and used software after I learned and used schematics, and have been left questioning for a long time why my hardware designs were more reliable than my software designs. Currently, I think that the difference is the asynchronous vs. synchronous mindset.

Hence, I think that we should cherry pick "the good parts" of schematics and attempt to implement and improve those ideas using whatever building materials we have access to (e.g. ICs, assembler, synchronous / sequential programming languages, CPUs, GPUs, FPGAs, etc.). I've managed to do this in Common Lisp and Python. Zac at Kagi.com did this in Odin in about one person-week of work and got me hooked on using draw.io (and swearing at it - it's more like using Word to write code than having a really good programming editor :-).

Defining the "semantics" of components is easy - stimulus vs. response over time. Snapping components together is easy: queue classes, first-class functions, break the coupling due to call/block/return behaviour implicit in mapping "functions" onto CPU opcodes. Once this is done, making diagrams becomes trivial and easily mappable to lower-level code (like any modern function-based programming language, or assembler).


The BIG difference is that one notation is sequential / synchronous and the other notation is non-sequential / asynchronous. The former has lots of gotchas when it comes to dealing with any problem that falls outside of the functional paradigm and has been continuously patched and hacked for 50+ years to make up for its deficiencies. The latter had Moore's Law and thrived. 



My hard-core exposure to electronics began 50+ years ago, when resistors, capacitors and inductors were affordable, and vacuum tubes were affordable only to parents, not students. I wasn't aware of semiconductor devices, like transistors and diodes until later. ICs (integrated circuits containing more than one transistor per package) entered my awareness only later.

Electrical devices were characterized for their physical, time-related(!!!) properties by the likes of Steinmetz, Heaviside, and others before them. (aside: as far as I know, Maxwell's equations were originally written as quaternions, and Heaviside's re-casting of them left out some aspects, hence, Maxwell's equations are useful but not definitive (I'm not sure about this - Claude says I'm wrong, but is trained only on consensus physics))

Because electrical components were actual physical devices, completely isolated from one another, "schematics" could be drawn to show how they were connected into circuits, with different symbols for each kind of thing. Claude claims that standardization of these symbols came at about the same time as FORTRAN was invented (appendix below).

The fact that electrical components were utterly isolated from one another meant that you could pop them out of a circuit and reuse them elsewhere, regardless of the number of input and output ports. Functional programming calls this feat "referential transparency", but restricts it to one input (a block of data that comes all at once and can be further destructured into several sub-types) and one output. The other common output - stderr and exceptions - is a hack patched in to cover for the fact that pure functionalism isn't good enough to describe the workings of physical devices.

Each element in a schematic ran at its own speed, i.e. asynchronous and parallel. Electrical engineers needed to take this into account - and managed to do so without too much hand-wringing.

The first ICs were simply collections of transistors driven to the max (0v and 5v - "digital"). Think Jimi Hendrix overdriving his Marshal stacks vs. clean-sounding jazz players like Django Reinhardt. Along with this trick came various strategies about dealing with "digital" electronics, like de Morgan's Laws, etc.

Digital circuits became more and more complex. More and more transistors encased in epoxy (usually black, sometimes gray, sometimes ceramic instead of epoxy and plastic :-).

Then, someone figured out that you could provide a bank of circuits inside a single IC and give each sub-circuit a binary "code", and, if you bolted this kind of IC to another kind of IC that contained "memory" circuits you could make field-customizable, re-programmable ICs. 

So, the range of ICs available to circuit designers was greatly expanded, as long as the designers were willing to put up with arranging codes in certain sequences within such "CPU" ICs.

Back in 1972, Atari released a game called "Pong" and made a lot of money with it. The original design contained only non-reprogrammable ICs. Later, Atari engineers tried to make the Pong devices cheaper to manufacture by replacing groups of the non-reprogrammable ICs with fewer reprogrammable ICs.

Meanwhile, groups of researchers tried to figure out ways to make reprogrammable ICs easier to reprogram. The mathematical community inserted itself into this line of thinking and bent "programming languages" towards the problem of evaluating mathematical equations using reprogrammable ICs, since it was deemed obvious that the only use for these things was to calculate missile trajectories, de-employing the banks of human calculators who were doing that kind of thing at the time and calculating compound interest for banking institutions and various Ponzi schemes. [Arawjo](https://ianarawjo.therottingcartridge.com/docs/To_Write_Code_Arawjo_CHI2020.pdf) discusses some of the other alternate notation possibilities available at the time.

Since then, we’ve been ad-hoc patching function-based computational programming languages for creating other kinds of uses - like sequencers (DAWs, iMovie, etc) and large-distance (> 1nm) distributed systems. Meanwhile, the hardware community preserved the asynchronous, physical nature of electrical devices and ICs and achieved rapid growth and rode out Moore’s Law without much hand-wringing.

IMO, a CPU is just another kind of IC that can be used as a building material to build interesting devices. The way the innards of an IC work (synchronous / sequential recipes of opcodes) is valid only for thinking about using such ICs, but is not necessarily the best way to think about other devices that make up reprogrammable machines (aka "computers"). This opinion is bolstered every time we get surprised by issues such as callback hell or Mars Pathfinder failures.

McCarthy's Lisp 1.5 is "functional programming". Sector Lisp is its successor. Turing Machines are not "functional programming". If you want assignment, then you shouldn't be using the "functional programming" paradigm. Given that physical devices in reality possess state, we _should_ want something further than "functional programming". Functional programming is a useful spherical cow, but we need to recognize when to move on to using other paradigms in conjunction with functional programming.

The fact that we _can_ hack assignment and LLMs into the functional paradigm is not a strength of the paradigm. It is but a testament to human will power.

"Standing on the shoulders of giants" is a good tactic, as long as those giants were solving problems that matter in the 21st century. They weren't, they were solving single-CPU, single-thread problem. We need new shoulders.

**Your question:** _When did electric circuit diagrams appear?_

---

The answer has two layers — when circuits were first drawn, and when standardized schematic notation appeared.

**The drawings came first, roughly the early-to-mid 19th century.** Georg Ohm mathematically analysed the electrical circuit in 1827, and diagrams of some kind were necessary to communicate that work. But these were closer to physical sketches than standardized schematics — drawings of apparatus, not abstract symbolic notation. Simple components often had symbols intended to represent some feature of the physical construction of the device — the resistor symbol, for instance, derived from the physical appearance of wirewound resistors.

**Standardized schematic notation came later, late 19th into early 20th century.** It wasn't until the late 19th and early 20th centuries that standardization began to take place, driven by organizations such as the International Electrotechnical Commission, the IEEE, and ANSI. These diagrams have been around for over a century, dating back to United States patents for some of the earliest electronic devices.

**The relevance to your argument**

The timeline is striking. Standardized circuit diagram notation — the diagrammatic language that gave us the async black-box composition model, that eventually produced CPUs — is roughly contemporary with FORTRAN and the early synchronous/sequential programming languages. Both notations emerged in the same historical window, mid-20th century, from the same hardware reality. Hardware engineers chose the diagrammatic path and got Moore's Law. Software engineers chose the textual/sequential path and got callback hell. The choice was made in the same era, under the same constraints, and produced radically different trajectories.

**Your question:** _Physics has a fundamental equation e = ½mv². There is an equation of similar shape in electrical analysis. What is it?_

---

There are actually two, and together they form a complementary pair:

**E = ½LI²** — energy stored in an inductor, where L is inductance and I is current.

**E = ½CV²** — energy stored in a capacitor, where C is capacitance and V is voltage.

Both have the identical ½ × (property) × (quantity)² shape as kinetic energy.

The analogy runs deeper than shape. There is a precise structural mapping between mechanical and electrical systems:

|Mechanical|Electrical|
|---|---|
|mass (m)|inductance (L)|
|velocity (v)|current (I)|
|spring constant (1/k)|capacitance (C)|
|displacement (x)|charge (Q)|
|force (F)|voltage (V)|
|damping|resistance|

So ½LI² is the direct analogue of ½mv² — inductance resists changes in current the same way mass resists changes in velocity. And ½CV² is the analogue of spring potential energy ½kx².

This mapping is not cosmetic. An LC circuit (inductor + capacitor) oscillates for exactly the same mathematical reason a mass on a spring oscillates — they are governed by the same differential equation. The physics is identical; only the substrate differs.

The reason this is worth noting in the context of your broader argument: the electrical world already had a complete, formal, and mathematically rigorous framework for reasoning about dynamic systems with time, energy, and state — before digital computers existed. That framework was expressed in circuit diagrams. Software inherited almost none of it.
