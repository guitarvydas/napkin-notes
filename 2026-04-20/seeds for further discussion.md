The second, larger idea is that we are still in an early, infatuated phase with general-purpose Turing-complete computation — treating the machinery as the point rather than as a means to an end. The suggestion is that computation will eventually be reabsorbed into devices as an unremarkable implementation detail, the way electric motors are now unremarkable inside appliances. When that happens, "how much code" becomes a poor measure of complexity, and better measures — presumably around fitness for purpose, expressiveness per domain, or semantic density — become possible. The aside about counting microcode is a pointed reminder that the boundary between "code" and "hardware" is already arbitrary and politically managed — Intel's microcode is code, just code we are not supposed to count.

Together the two ideas rhyme with your own position: that the synchronous/sequential programming paradigm is building material mistaken for universal principle, and that the right measure of a notation is not its generality but its fit to the problem at hand.
