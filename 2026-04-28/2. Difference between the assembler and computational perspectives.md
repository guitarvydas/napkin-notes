*what is the difference between the assembler and computational perspectives?*

One sentence: **computability theory asks "what?" and assembler asks "how much?"**

But the difference runs deeper than just cost.

**Computability is timeless.**

It exists outside of physics. A Turing machine has infinite tape, unlimited time, no energy cost. Computation in this frame is a mathematical object — a function from inputs to outputs. The _process_ of getting there is irrelevant. There is no "meanwhile."

**Assembler is embedded in physics.**

Every instruction takes cycles. Every cycle is wall-clock time. Memory has latency. Interrupts arrive from the outside world on their own schedule, not yours. The program is not a mathematical function — it is a physical process unfolding in real time alongside other physical processes.

**The computability perspective treats computation as a closed system.**

Input goes in, output comes out, nothing else matters. This is fine for batch problems — sorting a list, computing a hash, proving a theorem. The world waits.

**The assembler perspective treats computation as an open system.**

The program is embedded in an environment that keeps happening whether the program is ready or not. Interrupts, timers, I/O, other CPUs — these don't wait for your function to return.

**This is exactly Prigogine's distinction.**

Closed systems tend to equilibrium — to the "correct answer." Open systems are driven by flows from outside and never reach equilibrium. They produce structure _through_ time, not _despite_ it. Computability theory models closed systems. Real hardware — and real software — is an open system.

**The calling convention is the symptom.**

It assumes the world pauses while you traverse a call stack. Assembler, and Forth, and your 0D/PBP model, refuse that assumption. The calling convention is the language's way of pretending the system is closed.

The assembler perspective is, at bottom, a thermodynamic perspective. The computational perspective is a mathematical one. The tragedy of modern programming is that we built our languages on the mathematics and then wondered why they couldn't model the thermodynamics.
