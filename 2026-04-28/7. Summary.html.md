<h1>The Calling Convention Is the Architecture</h1>

<hr>

<h2>TL;DR</h2>

<p>Assembler and computability theory are not two views of the same thing. They are two different questions. Computability asks what can be computed. Assembler asks what it costs. Modern programming languages answer the first question and pretend the second doesn't exist. Continuous Deployment is what happens when the second question reasserts itself in production, at the customer's expense.</p>

<hr>

<h2>L;R</h2>

<h2>What Assembler Can Express That No HLL Can</h2>

<p>The standard answer is: nothing. Any Turing-complete language can compute anything assembler can compute. This is true and nearly useless.</p>

<p>The better answer is about control, not capability. Assembler gives you direct access to the hardware's actual model: registers, flags, the stack as a data structure you own, interrupts as first-class events, cycle-level timing. A higher-level language doesn't just hide these things — it replaces the hardware's model with a different model. That replacement is not neutral. It has costs that cannot be compiled away.</p>

<p>The clearest example is Forth. Forth's inner interpreter — the NEXT macro — is two or three instructions: load the next word address, jump to it. No frame. No saved registers. No return address on the system stack. Forth keeps its own instruction pointer, its own parameter stack, its own return stack, all in dedicated CPU registers. The calling convention is NEXT. That's it.</p>

<p>A language with a standard calling convention cannot express this without penalty. The convention owns certain registers. The compiler assumes it can use them. You cannot permanently dedicate them to Forth's VM state without fighting the compiler for control of the machine. Even GCC's computed-goto tricks cannot guarantee that your dedicated registers won't be spilled across a boundary. The subroutine-threaded Forth that C forces you toward replaces NEXT with CALL/RET — strictly heavier, because CALL pushes a return address onto the system stack, RET pops it, and the processor's branch predictor gets involved.</p>

<p>The calling convention is not a detail. It is the language's implicit claim about what a computation transition costs. Forth's claim is: one indirect jump. C's claim is: a frame. You cannot make C's hardware do Forth's accounting.</p>

<h2>The Two Questions</h2>

<p>Computability theory is timeless. It exists outside of physics. A Turing machine has infinite tape, unlimited time, no energy cost. Computation in this frame is a mathematical object — a function from inputs to outputs. The process of getting there is irrelevant. There is no meanwhile.</p>

<p>Assembler is embedded in physics. Every instruction takes cycles. Every cycle is wall-clock time. Memory has latency. Interrupts arrive from the outside world on their own schedule, not yours. The program is not a mathematical function. It is a physical process unfolding in real time alongside other physical processes.</p>

<p>Computability models closed systems. Input arrives, computation runs, output is produced, the world waited. Assembler models open systems. The program is embedded in an environment that keeps happening whether the program is ready or not. This is not a minor difference. It is the difference between mathematics and thermodynamics.</p>

<p>The calling convention is the symptom. It assumes the world pauses while you traverse a call stack. The hardware knows this is false. Interrupts don't wait for your function to return. The CPU has been asynchronous at the silicon level since the beginning. The languages refused to acknowledge it.</p>

<h2>What Opcode Scripts Reveal</h2>

<p>Raw opcode sequences expose the hardware's actual theory of computation: state rather than hierarchy, no privileged notion of call, interrupts as first-class citizens, time as a real quantity. A script of opcodes can implement Forth's threading model, or coroutines, or a state machine, or an Actor system — any execution model you choose — without any one of them being privileged by the notation.</p>

<p>A higher-level language is not just an abstraction over opcodes. It is an opinion about what computation is, enforced syntactically. That opinion was formed in the era of the printing press and the teletype, when the only available notation was a line of text read left to right. The sequential, hierarchical, call/return model is a notation artifact. It is not a property of the hardware.</p>

<p>The hardware has been telling us something about asynchrony for seventy years. The languages declined to listen.</p>

<h2>What Computability Cannot Say About Async Systems</h2>

<p>A Turing machine can simulate any network of asynchronous processes. This is the universality result, and it is correct. But simulation is not expression. The TM that simulates a network of async processes is a sequential encoding of something that is not sequential. The topology — who connects to whom, via which ports, with what message semantics — has been compiled away into the tape. It no longer exists as a first-class object in the formalism. You cannot ask the TM what the architecture of the system it is simulating is. That information is gone.</p>

<p>Computability has no vocabulary for topology, simultaneity, or open-system composition. A system of async processes is not a function. It has no output in the mathematical sense. It has ongoing behavior — a stream of reactions to a stream of stimuli, extending indefinitely in time, with the structure of those reactions depending on the wiring of components as much as on their individual computations.</p>

<p>Process calculi — CSP, CCS, the pi-calculus, the Actor model — were invented precisely because computability lacked this vocabulary. They introduce channels, synchronization operators, parallel composition. They can express topology. But deciding non-trivial properties of systems built from these primitives is in general undecidable, and they still struggle with genuinely open systems where the environment is not modeled in advance.</p>

<p>Computability can tell you whether a single process halts. It cannot tell you what a network of non-halting reactive processes does, because what it does is not a function from input to output. It is a structure in time. And computability theory has no clock.</p>

<h2>Sutherland and Thompson</h2>

<p>Sketchpad was written by Ivan Sutherland over the course of his PhD at MIT, completed in 1963. Ken Thompson wrote the first version of Unix in three weeks in 1969, spending roughly one week each on the kernel, the shell, an editor, and an assembler. Both worked in the language of the machine being programmed. Both had radical scope discipline. Both held the entire design in a single mind without committee negotiation or specification documents. The design was the implementation. There was no gap between architecture and code because they were the same artifact.</p>

<p>Thompson had spent years on Multics before touching the PDP-7. He knew precisely what he did not want. The three weeks was not the project. It was the precipitation of crystallized understanding onto a machine with thirty-one instructions and 4K of 18-bit words. The hardware was simple enough that one person could hold it completely. The scope was small enough that nothing was hidden. The notation — PDP-7 assembly — matched the target without impedance.</p>

<p>Modern software development reverses every one of these conditions. The notation fights the target. The scope is unbounded. The design is distributed across teams, tickets, and documents that no single person can hold. The productivity of Thompson and Sutherland is not a historical curiosity. It is a measurement of everything we added afterward and its cost.</p>

<h2>Continuous Deployment Is Not a Methodology</h2>

<p>Continuous Deployment exists because our notations are not expressive enough to reason about systems before running them. If you cannot think clearly about a system's behavior in advance, you discover its behavior empirically, in production. CD is the industrialization of that discovery process.</p>

<p>The customer wanted something. The developer built something. They differed. Instead of addressing why they differed — notation failure, communication failure, specification failure — the industry invented a process for iteratively converging on what was wanted by repeatedly failing in production. Then celebrated it as agility.</p>

<p>The developer's cost of CD is visible and manageable: automated pipelines, rollback mechanisms, feature flags. The customer's cost is externalized and largely invisible. Workflows break mid-task. Data is migrated without consent. Learned behavior is invalidated overnight. Time is spent relearning systems that changed without notice. Trust erodes. The customer bears the cost of the developer's incomplete understanding. This is not a minor inefficiency. It is a systematic transfer of failure cost from producer to consumer, dressed in the language of responsiveness.</p>

<p>The Agile movement reframed the inability to specify correctly as a virtue. Embrace change. Respond to feedback. Working software over comprehensive documentation. These are reasonable reactions to waterfall's rigidity. They overcorrected into normalizing perpetual incompleteness. The sprint cycle is an admission that nobody knows what they're building, formalized into a ritual. The customer became a bug-finder on a treadmill they did not choose to board.</p>

<p>A notation expressive enough to capture what the customer wanted before deployment would make CD not just unnecessary but recognizable as the malpractice it is. The fact that we do not have such a notation — or have not built it — is not an argument for CD. It is the problem that should have been solved first.</p>

<p>Thompson wrote Unix in three weeks because he knew exactly what he wanted to build and had the notation to build it directly. He did not need to continuously deploy to Ken Thompson to discover what Ken Thompson wanted.</p>

<p>The calling convention is the architecture. The notation is the theory. The theory determines what can be thought before deployment. What cannot be thought before deployment gets discovered in production. The customer pays for the thinking we did not do.</p>
