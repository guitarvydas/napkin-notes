*Can computability express asynchronous processes? It can express how to build async processes and how to build reactive processes, but can it express how a system built of such processes be constructed?*

No. And this is a precise gap, not a vague one.

**What computability can express:**

A Turing machine can _simulate_ any async process, and any network of async processes. This is the universality result. Given enough tape and enough time, a TM can produce the same outputs as any system of communicating processes.

But simulation is not expression. The TM that simulates a network of async processes is a _sequential encoding_ of something that is fundamentally not sequential. The topology — who is connected to whom, via which ports, with what message semantics — has been _compiled away_ into the tape. It no longer exists as a first-class object in the formalism. You cannot ask the TM "what is the architecture of the system you are simulating?" It has no answer. That information is gone.

**The specific things computability cannot express:**

- **Topology as a first-class object.** Which component sends to which, via which channel. Computability has no vocabulary for this.
- **Simultaneity.** Multiple processes existing and reacting _at the same time_. A TM has one head, one state, one step. Concurrency must be serialized to fit the model, which destroys the thing being modeled.
- **Open-system composition.** A system that reacts to an environment that keeps changing _while the system runs_. Computability models closed systems — input arrives, computation happens, output produced. The idea of a process that is _perpetually_ waiting, reacting, and continuing is not a natural object in the formalism.
- **The architecture itself as a computable object.** You can write a program that builds async processes. You cannot write, in the computability framework, a statement _about_ the emergent behavior of a _particular wiring_ of such processes — because the wiring is not a first-class entity.

**Why process calculi don't fully solve this:**

CSP, CCS, the pi-calculus, the Actor model — these were invented _precisely because_ computability theory lacked the vocabulary. They introduce new primitives: channels, synchronization operators, parallel composition. They can express topology. But they still face a wall: deciding non-trivial properties of systems built from these primitives is in general undecidable — and worse than classical undecidability, because now you have undecidability _plus_ unbounded interaction. And they still struggle with genuinely open systems where the environment is not modeled in advance.

**The deepest point:**

Computability theory inherits the closed-system, function-in/value-out model from mathematics. A function has a domain and a codomain. Computation is the process of getting from one to the other. This is a fine model for batch problems.

A system of async processes is not a function. It has no "output" in the mathematical sense. It has _ongoing behavior_ — a stream of reactions to a stream of stimuli, extending indefinitely in time, with the structure of the reactions depending on the _wiring_ of the components as much as on their individual computations.

This is precisely what your 0D/PBP model is pointing at. The components are topology-blind — they don't know their neighbors — _because the topology is not part of the computation, it is part of the architecture_. Computability theory conflates these two things by encoding everything onto a tape. PBP keeps them separate, which is the right move, because they _are_ separate.

Computability can tell you whether a single process halts. It cannot tell you what a network of non-halting reactive processes _does_ — because "what it does" is not a function from input to output. It is a structure in time. And computability theory, like the HLLs built on it, has no clock.
