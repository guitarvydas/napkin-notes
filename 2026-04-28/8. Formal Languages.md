The Slack link requires authentication — I can’t reach it. But your summary of the paper’s claims is detailed enough to respond to everything you’ve raised.
On the UNIX/C claim.
You’re correct. Ken Thompson wrote Unix in PDP-7 assembly language. ￼ The first version of Unix on the PDP-11/20 also used PDP-11/20 assembly language, and Unix was rewritten in C only in 1972. ￼ So the claim that C enabled the creation of Unix inverts the history. Unix enabled the creation of C. The paper has the causality backwards.
On “theoretical” vs “concrete” languages.
The distinction as described is doing real work but is under-defined in a way that creates the problem you’ve identified. The commenter’s refinement — theoretical languages for theorems, concrete languages for running programs — is cleaner but still leaves assembler in a strange position.
Here is what I think is actually going on. The paper is implicitly using a three-way distinction while only naming two categories:
Mathematical notation (lambda calculus, predicate logic) — formal, no operational semantics intended, exists to support reasoning.
High-level programming languages (what the paper calls “concrete”) — formal, operational, abstracted above the machine.
Machine-level notation (assembler, machine code, opcodes) — formal, operational, but directly expressive of the hardware’s model rather than abstracting above it.
The paper collapses the second and third into “concrete” because both run on computers. But this conflates two entirely different relationships to the machine. A HLL abstracts the machine away. Assembler is the machine’s own notation. These are not the same kind of thing.
On whether assembler is “informal.”
It is not. Assembler is maximally formal in the operationally relevant sense — every instruction has a completely specified effect on machine state. There is no ambiguity, no interpretation, no runtime that mediates between notation and execution. The instruction is the behavior.
If anything, assembler is more formal than a HLL in the sense that matters for computation — there is no layer of translation that could introduce discrepancy between what is written and what executes. The HLL depends on a compiler whose correctness is assumed but not guaranteed. Assembler depends on nothing but the hardware specification.
Sutherland was not being ad hoc. He was being precisely formal in the language that most directly expressed the machine he was programming. The TX-2 was the specification. His assembler code was the formal expression of the intended behavior of that machine. The fact that modern standards no longer call this “formal” reflects the narrowing of that term to mean “uses lambda calculus or type theory,” not anything about rigor or precision.
On the DNA/RNA analogy — the most interesting point you’ve raised.
This is pointing at something real and underappreciated. The transition from transistors to machine code involved exactly what you describe: lassoing behavior into named, reusable chunks (subroutines) that could be invoked by a simple mechanism (the call instruction / return address convention). The subroutine is a strand of behavior that a small interpreter walks. The CPU is the barnacle that executes it.
Forth extended this explicitly. A Forth word is a named sequence of other words. The inner interpreter (NEXT) walks the sequence. Words are strands of DNA. NEXT is the ribosome reading them. The analogy is not metaphorical — it is structural.
The question you’re asking is whether we can apply the same move again — find the current level of complexity, lasso it into named chunks, and build a new layer of “barnacle processors” that walk and interpret those chunks. This is precisely what PBP/0D is doing. A Part is a named chunk of reactive behavior. The dispatcher that routes messages between Parts is the new NEXT. The topology is the strand being walked.
The pattern is: find the irreducible unit of behavior at the current level, name it, make it composable, build a minimal interpreter that walks compositions. Repeat. Each application of this pattern vaults one level of complexity. We applied it from transistors to gates, gates to machine code, machine code to subroutines, subroutines to processes. The next vault is from processes to reactive component networks with explicit topology.
On the conclusion.
You are right that it doesn’t follow from the paper’s own argument. If everything executable by a computer is a formal language — which is true by any reasonable definition — then the distinction between “theoretical” and “concrete” formal languages cannot be the conclusion. It must be a premise or a framing device. Calling it a conclusion suggests the paper is working backwards from a desired distinction rather than deriving it.
The paper seems to want to say something like: “the languages we use for implementation have drifted from the languages we use for reasoning, and this is a problem.” That is a real and important claim. But it requires the third category — machine-level notation — to be acknowledged, not hidden inside “concrete,” or the history of Unix and Sketchpad becomes an embarrassment rather than an exemplar.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

The HAL archive is bot-protected and won’t serve the PDF directly. But the abstract and the snippet retrieved from the search results give enough to work with — and the snippet actually reveals something important that sharpens your critique.
What the abstract actually says the distinction is.
By “programming languages” are meant both concrete languages and abstract computation models. However, the formers have many properties which the latters try to avoid, and which are close to those of natural languages: syntactic irregularities, historical residues, expletive or redundant symbols, learning through practice. ￼
So Mélès’s actual distinction is: concrete languages (things like C, Forth, Python) vs abstract computation models (lambda calculus, Turing machines, register machines). The translation in your Slack thread rendered “abstract computation models” as “theoretical languages” — which is a reasonable rendering but slightly loses the precision. The distinction is not theoretical/concrete but rather model-of-computation vs implemented-language.
This reframes your objection somewhat. Assembler is unambiguously a concrete language under this definition — it is implemented, it runs on real hardware, it has historical residues and irregularities. It is not an abstract computation model. So the paper’s distinction does not classify assembler as informal or as a third category. The translation you were reading may have introduced the confusion.
But your deeper critique still stands.
The paper’s distinction, even correctly stated, does not address the question of what kind of concrete language assembler is relative to HLLs. It lumps Sketchpad’s TX-2 assembler and modern Python into the same category — “concrete language” — and the philological apparatus (comparative analysis, diachrony, etymology) treats them as things to be classified historically rather than evaluated for expressive power relative to the machine they target.
The retrieved snippet reveals something directly relevant to our earlier discussion.
The computability argument forgets that there is no absolute equivalence. Like any equivalence judgment, Turing equivalence depends on a congruence relation, whose relevance anyone wishing to avoid begging the question must evaluate before using it. Felleisen objects immediately that this criterion is too coarse to account for the informal notion of expressivity: it is pointless to compare the set of computable functions a language can represent because the languages in question are generally universal. ￼
Mélès is citing Felleisen’s expressivity argument — which maps directly onto the distinction we drew between computability (what?) and assembler (how much?). Mélès knows the Turing equivalence argument is too coarse. He is not making the naive claim that all languages are equivalent. The paper’s philological frame is precisely an attempt to find a finer-grained vocabulary for comparing languages that computability cannot provide.
This makes the paper more interesting than your Slack thread’s rendering suggested — and also makes your critique more pointed. If Mélès knows Turing equivalence is too coarse, and is reaching for philological methods as a finer instrument, then the missing third category — machine-level notation as a distinct kind of concrete language — is exactly the gap that philology alone cannot fill. Etymology and diachrony can tell you where C’s syntax came from. They cannot tell you what the calling convention costs.
On the conclusion.
Your objection stands regardless: if the paper’s own argument acknowledges that Turing equivalence is inadequate, then a conclusion that rests on the formal/informal distinction without addressing the cost dimension is incomplete. The philological frame is a useful tool for the history and sociology of programming languages. It is not sufficient for evaluating what a language can express about the hardware it runs on, or what it costs to run on that hardware. Those questions require the assembler perspective, not the philologist’s.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
