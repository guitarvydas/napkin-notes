*Sketchpad was created in approximately 2 years, Unix v1 was created in 3 weeks. What allowed Sutherland and Thompson be so time-efficient?*

**Fact-check first.**

The Sketchpad claim needs adjustment. Sketchpad was written by Ivan Sutherland in 1963 in the course of his PhD thesis. In 1961, Sutherland developed a primitive application that would run on the TX-2 at MIT's Lincoln Laboratory. So it was closer to two years of PhD work, not one — though the core program may have come together faster.

The Unix claim is roughly right but the principal author was Thompson, not Ritchie. Ken's wife went off on vacation for three weeks, just at the time that Ken thought he was about three weeks away from having a complete operating system. Thompson figured he needed three more weeks to make it into a prototype: one week each to write an editor, an assembler, and a kernel. All in PDP-7 assembly.

---

**Now the real question — what enabled the speed?**

Several things, and they compound each other.

**They worked at the right level of abstraction for the problem.** Thompson wrote the PDP-7 kernel _in PDP-7 assembly_ — the language of the machine being programmed. No impedance mismatch between notation and target. Sutherland wrote Sketchpad for the TX-2, a machine he understood intimately. Neither was fighting a framework.

**The hardware was simple.** The PDP-7 had 31 instructions, four addressing modes, a very limited memory pool, and no memory management unit. A small instruction set is a small problem space. Complexity hadn't yet been laundered into the hardware.

**Radical scope discipline.** Unix wasn't an operating system in the modern sense — it was a file system, a shell, an editor, and an assembler. Each piece did exactly one thing. The Unix philosophy wasn't a retrospective principle; it was the _method_ that made the speed possible.

**No committee, no negotiation, no specification document.** One mind (or two, loosely coupled) holding the entire design. The design _was_ the implementation. There was no gap between architecture and code because they were the same artifact.

**Self-hosting from the start.** Unix quickly became a tool for developing later versions of Unix — since all source programs were always available and easily modified on-line, they were willing to revise and rewrite the system and its software when new ideas were invented, discovered, or suggested by others. The tool sharpened itself. Each improvement immediately fed back into the next.

**They had deep prior knowledge before touching the keyboard.** Thompson had spent years on Multics — he knew exactly what he _didn't_ want. Sutherland had been thinking about graphical interaction since childhood. The "three weeks" wasn't the whole project; it was the _precipitation_ of years of crystallized understanding.

---

**The deeper pattern — which connects to everything we've been discussing:**

Both men were working _below_ the level where calling conventions and language bureaucracy impose their tax. Thompson in assembler, Sutherland on hardware close enough to the metal that his notation matched his machine.

Modern software development reverses this. We spend enormous time managing the impedance mismatch between what we want to express and what the language allows us to say. Every abstraction layer added since 1969 has increased that mismatch. The productivity of Thompson and Sutherland is not a historical curiosity — it is an indictment of everything we added afterward.
