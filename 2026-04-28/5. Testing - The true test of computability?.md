*Confirm or deny: the "true test" of computability is 0 defects in the field, one and only one deployment, no use of Continuous Deployment.*

Deny — but carefully, because the intuition behind it is pointing at something real.

**Why it fails as stated:**

Computability theory has no concept of defects, deployment, or continuous deployment. It is a mathematical framework about what is theoretically computable. It says nothing about correctness in the engineering sense, nothing about field behavior, nothing about release cadence. Claiming computability sets these standards is a category error — like saying Euclidean geometry requires zero-latency networks.

**What the intuition is actually pointing at:**

The claim seems to be reaching for something like: _a truly well-understood system should be correct before deployment, not corrected after._ That is an engineering philosophy, not a computability result. And as an engineering philosophy it has real teeth:

- Continuous deployment normalizes incomplete understanding. You ship, observe failures, patch. The system becomes the specification retroactively.
- "0 defects, one deployment" implies the system was _fully understood before it was built_ — that the design space was explored in notation, not in production.
- This is closer to how hardware works. You do not continuously deploy a CPU revision to customers and patch it in the field. You get it right in simulation and verification before tape-out, because the cost of field failure is prohibitive.

**Where the intuition connects to the real discussion:**

The deeper claim is that continuous deployment is a _symptom_ — evidence that our notations are not expressive enough to reason about systems before running them. If you cannot think clearly about a system's behavior in advance, you are forced to discover its behavior empirically, in production. CD is the industrialization of that failure.

This connects directly to the computability/async gap we just identified. If your notation cannot express the topology and behavior of a system of async processes, you _cannot_ reason about it before deployment. So you ship and patch. The patching is not a methodology — it is an admission that the notation failed.

**The sharper formulation:**

The true test is not computability. The true test is _expressibility_. Can your notation express the system's intended behavior precisely enough that correctness can be established before deployment? If yes, one deployment is achievable. If no, continuous deployment is inevitable — not as a choice, but as a consequence of the notation's inadequacy.

Computability is a ceiling. Expressibility is the actual constraint. And current languages and frameworks fail the expressibility test for async systems, which is why CD exists and is celebrated rather than recognized as the failure mode it represents.
