 contend that the existence of CD is admission of failure and should not be celebrated. It appears that computation expresses what the developer intended, but doesn't address what the final customer wanted. CD is a process - an expensive process for the final customer - of discovering, iteratively, what is wanted.*

Agreed completely. And the implications are more damning than they first appear.

**CD is requirements failure laundered into a deployment methodology.**

The customer wanted something. The developer built something. They differed. Instead of addressing _why_ they differed — notation failure, communication failure, specification failure — the industry invented a process for iteratively converging on what was wanted by repeatedly failing in production. Then celebrated it as agility.

This is not engineering. A bridge engineer does not continuously deploy bridge versions and observe which ones collapse.

**The cost is systematically hidden.**

The developer's cost of CD is visible and manageable — automated pipelines, rollback mechanisms, feature flags. The customer's cost is externalized and largely invisible:

- Workflows that break mid-task
- Data migrated without consent
- Learned behavior invalidated overnight
- Time spent relearning systems that changed without notice
- Trust eroded by instability

The customer bears the cost of the developer's incomplete understanding. This is not a minor inefficiency — it is a systematic transfer of failure cost from producer to consumer, dressed up as responsiveness.

**"Agile" made this structural.**

The Agile movement reframed the inability to specify correctly as a virtue — "embrace change," "respond to feedback," "working software over comprehensive documentation." These are reasonable reactions to waterfall's rigidity, but they overcorrected into normalizing perpetual incompleteness. The sprint cycle is an admission that nobody knows what they're building, formalized into a ritual.

The customer became a bug-finder on a treadmill they didn't choose to get on.

**What computability has to do with it:**

As we established — computability expresses what a program _does_ given inputs. It does not express what a customer _needs_ from a system embedded in their actual work and life. The gap between those two things is not a technical gap. It is a notation and communication gap. We have no formalism for capturing "what the customer wanted" that is precise enough to verify against, so we substitute "what we shipped" and iterate.

The existence of requirements languages, UML, use cases, user stories — all of these are attempts to bridge that gap. All have failed to formalize it precisely enough to close it before deployment. So CD fills the vacuum.

**The deeper indictment:**

CD treats the customer as a component in the development feedback loop — an input device for requirements discovery — rather than as the endpoint the entire process should serve. The customer is instrumentalized. Their pain during each iteration is treated as signal, not as harm.

A notation expressive enough to capture what the customer wanted before deployment would make CD not just unnecessary but recognized as the malpractice it is. The fact that we cannot build such a notation — or have not — is not an argument for CD. It is the problem that should have been solved first.

Thompson wrote Unix in three weeks because he knew exactly what he wanted to build and had the notation to build it. He did not need to continuously deploy to Ken Thompson to discover what Ken Thompson wanted.
