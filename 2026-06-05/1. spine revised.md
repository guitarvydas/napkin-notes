Good — that substantially reshapes the "function calls" section. Let me restate the spine with your nuance folded in before drafting:

**Opening:** The LaserWriter cable. Two architectures visible in hardware. Centralized vs decentralized.

**The wrong starting point:** Functional paradigm comes from mathematics. Early computing mistook RMs (Reprogrammable Machines) for functional-paradigm machines. RMs can do time-related things — clearly outside the functional sweet spot. Nobody stopped to ask whether mathematical notation was the right notation for a new medium.

**Decades of bandaids:** Every use-case outside the functional sweet spot produced a workaround. SIMD: wider registers to handle parallel calculations. SIMT: looser coupling for more parallelism. LLM hardware: kludging tortured architectures further. Each iteration more complicated, harder to reason about, harder to get correct.

**Three specific mechanisms — each a workaround, not a design decision:**

- Shared memory: a speed optimization for shorter communication paths, mistaken for a first-class design principle. Internet systems have multiple paths; we ignored them.
- The scheduler: scaffolding required because programs are composed of tightly-coupled function chains. A different notation might not need it.
- The call stack: a memory optimization from the era of expensive memory. Invented to deduplicate code. Then inverted by the "inline" compiler optimization — unfolding the very thing the call stack folded.

**The right direction:** Start with loosely coupled async CPUs. Tighten only for specific cases. Treat centralization as an optimization, not a default.

**Closing anchor:** The LaserWriter knew this in 1985. Message passing. Three pins.
