# Software Development Has a Brainstorming Problem

_And what Failure Driven Development might do about it_

---

Every serious creative field has developed a rich pedagogy around brainstorming — the generative phase of work where raw material is produced before the evaluating mind gets involved. Songwriting has Pat Pattison. Fiction writing has Natalie Goldberg and Julia Cameron. Software development has... sprint planning?

This gap is real, and it matters. Here's a tour of what the other fields figured out, why software missed it, and why Failure Driven Development might be the framework that finally closes the gap.

---

## TL;DR

The songwriting and writing teachers all share one core move: make it safe to produce bad output. Software culture has never institutionalized this. FDD reframes failure as the mechanism of discovery — which is exactly the philosophical infrastructure the generative phase of programming has been missing.

## L;R

### What Songwriting Teachers Figured Out

Pat Pattison, the Berklee College of Music professor whose students include John Mayer and Gillian Welch, built his brainstorming pedagogy around a technique he calls **object writing**. The exercise is simple: pick an object — any person, place, or thing — set a timer for ten minutes, and write about it using all seven senses. The five familiar ones plus _organic sense_ (how your body feels internally — butterflies in your stomach) and _kinesthetic sense_ (how your body moves in relation to space — the room was spinning). The rule is no self-editing, no crossing out, and stopping when the timer goes off regardless of where you are.

The deeper principle is what Pattison calls "the art of diving." In daily life we skim the surface of experience. Object writing forces a descent into concrete sensory detail — the kind of specific, embodied language that listeners can actually share. Abstract emotion becomes communicable only when grounded in sensory particulars.

He also teaches the title as a brainstorming scaffold: a strong title carries implicit "DNA" — a set of concepts and promises — that organizes what the verses can explore. And during the generative phase, the rule is an explicit "no free zone": the inner critic is told to stand down entirely.

Other songwriting teachers work adjacent territory. Jason Blume mines everyday conversation and overheard phrases for title ideas. Ralph Murphy (ASCAP) starts with the title and asks what promise it makes to the listener, then brainstorms only what fulfills that promise. Jimmy Webb (_Tunesmith_) is more romantic about it — walks, drives, solitude, boredom — creating conditions for the unconscious to work rather than following a system. Sheila Davis (_The Songwriter's Idea Book_) is the most toolkit-heavy: forty distinct strategies including clustering, mind-mapping, "what if" scenarios, and working from photographs.

The consensus across all of them: write before you edit, collect more than you need, and anchor abstract emotion in concrete specific detail.

### What Writing Teachers Figured Out

The fiction and nonfiction world arrived at essentially the same pedagogy independently.

Natalie Goldberg (_Writing Down the Bones_) is the closest to Pattison in spirit: timed "writing practice," keep your hand moving, don't cross out, go for the jugular. Very Zen-influenced — she treats writing practice as a form of meditation.

Julia Cameron (_The Artist's Way_) prescribes Morning Pages: three longhand pages every morning, stream of consciousness, no re-reading. The explicit goal is not good writing but drainage — clearing the "brain sludge" so the real creative work can flow later.

Peter Elbow (_Writing Without Teachers_) coined "freewriting" as a formal technique and articulated perhaps the clearest theoretical framing: the _generating self_ and the _evaluating self_ cannot operate simultaneously. They must be sequenced, not interleaved.

Anne Lamott (_Bird by Bird_) gives permission with her concept of the "shitty first draft" — the psychological license to write badly is the whole game. Also teaches radical scope narrowing: don't write a book, write what you can see through a one-inch picture frame.

Dorothea Brande (_Becoming a Writer_, 1934) is arguably the original: write immediately upon waking before the rational mind engages. She predates Cameron by fifty years with essentially the same insight.

### Why Software Missed This

The dominant culture treats programming as engineering or mathematics — fields where rigor and correctness are the virtues, not generative looseness. The inner critic is baked into the tools: the compiler rejects your shitty first draft immediately. It's structurally difficult to maintain "keep your hand moving, don't cross out" when the environment is constantly evaluating you.

The field also conflates _design_ brainstorming with _implementation_. Whiteboard sessions, user stories, sprint planning — these exist, but they're about requirements and architecture, not about the kind of raw generative thinking Pattison or Goldberg are after.

What does exist in software: sketching and paper prototyping in UX (borrowed from design thinking), "spike" solutions as throwaway exploratory code, rubber duck debugging as dialogic thinking-aloud, Ward Cunningham's CRC cards for object brainstorming. These are useful but ad hoc — nobody has assembled them into a coherent pedagogy.

What's entirely missing: a daily timed practice for generating design ideas, uncoupled from correctness. Nobody teaches programmers to separate the generating self from the evaluating self the way Elbow does explicitly. Nobody has articulated the software equivalent of object writing.

### Where FDD Comes In

Failure Driven Development reframes failure as information rather than catastrophe. That is exactly the philosophical move the other fields make to enable generative work — they just make it at the psychological level rather than the architectural one.

Lamott's shitty first drafts, Pattison's no-self-editing rule, Cameron's prohibition on re-reading morning pages — all of these are systems for making failure safe enough to be productive. FDD says the same thing at a deeper level: failure is not a deviation from the process, it's the mechanism of the process.

The songwriting and writing teachers have practice without theory — they know the techniques work but haven't fully articulated why. FDD is positioned to supply the theoretical grounding: the connection between psychological safety-to-fail and actual generative productivity is implicit in Goldberg and Pattison but never made explicit.

FDD could be the first framework in software that:

- Names the generating/evaluating split explicitly in a programming context
- Provides a concrete practice (analogous to object writing or morning pages) for the generative phase
- Grounds it in a coherent epistemology rather than just craft wisdom
- Distinguishes tactical failure-tolerance (agile's "iterate fast") from epistemic failure-embrace ("failure is how you find out what you need to know")

That last distinction is the one that makes FDD genuinely new rather than just a restatement of agile. Agile tolerates failure as an unfortunate cost of moving quickly. FDD treats failure as the primary signal — the thing that tells you where the real problem lives.

**Paul:** how does pat pattison teach brainstorming for songwriters

**Claude:** Pattison's core brainstorming method is called **object writing**, and it's the centerpiece of his book _Writing Better Lyrics_ (1995). Here's how it works:

**The basic exercise**

Pick a tangible object — a person, place, or thing — and do a timed free-write about it, keeping the writing anchored to all seven senses. Set a timer for 10 minutes, write whatever sensory impressions come to mind in a stream-of-consciousness style, and stop when the timer goes off. Don't write in complete sentences, and above all don't self-edit. If your mind veers off in different directions, go with it — as long as it stays sense-bound.

**The seven senses**

The five familiar senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) plus two less obvious ones: organic sense (how your body feels internally — "butterflies in your stomach") and kinesthetic sense (how your body moves in or relates to space — "the room was spinning").

**Why sense-bound writing specifically**

In daily life we mostly stay at surface level. In object writing, your job is "to master the art of diving" — getting to the bottom of the sea, finding the shell, opening it, and finding the pearl inside. The idea is that abstract emotions become communicable only when grounded in concrete sensory experience that listeners can share.

**The daily practice angle**

Pattison advises setting your alarm 10 minutes earlier and doing object writing first thing in the morning. He warns against going over 10 minutes — if you use a longer session one day, you'll use it as an excuse to skip the next day.

**The title as brainstorming tool**

Separate from object writing, Pattison also teaches using a title or hook as a brainstorming scaffold — it immediately suggests implicit concepts (the "DNA" of the title), and helps organize ideas into effective sections, making the process efficient while giving structure to what the verses can explore.

**The "no free zone" for generating raw material**

In his co-writing and classroom work, Pattison emphasizes a "NO-free zone" during the generative phase — no criticism, no shooting down ideas. The inner critic is explicitly told to stand down while material is being produced.

In short, his brainstorming philosophy is: bypass the conscious, judgmental mind by flooding the page with sense-specific concrete detail on a tight timer, then mine that raw material for lyric ideas.

**Paul:** any songwriting guru

**Claude:** Here are some of the major songwriting teachers and how they approach brainstorming/idea generation:

**Pat Pattison** (Berklee) — object writing, as covered above. Sense-bound timed free-writing to bypass the conscious mind.

**Jason Blume** — teaches a "title first" approach. Mine everyday conversation, overheard phrases, and clichés with a twist for title ideas. Keep a running list at all times; ideas are everywhere.

**John Braheny** (_The Craft and Business of Songwriting_) — emphasizes keeping an "idea file" — scraps of paper, recordings, journal entries — and revisiting them regularly. Raw material accumulates; you mine it later.

**Ralph Murphy** (ASCAP) — very Nashville-practical: start with the title, ask "what does this title promise the listener?" and brainstorm only what fulfills that promise. Ruthlessly cuts anything that doesn't serve the title's implied contract.

**Jimmy Webb** (_Tunesmith_) — more organic and romantic about it: walks, drives, daydreaming, letting the unconscious work. Less systematic than Pattison, more about creating conditions for inspiration — solitude, boredom, sensory experience.

**Sheila Davis** (_The Songwriter's Idea Book_) — the most explicitly brainstorm-focused of the lot. Forty distinct strategies including free association, clustering/mind-mapping around a central word, "what if" scenarios, and working from images or photographs.

**Berkeley's Andrea Stolpe** — teaches brainstorming from personal story: interview yourself about a specific emotional memory, extract concrete details, then find the universal in the specific.

The broad consensus across all of them: write before you edit, collect more than you need, and anchor abstract emotion in concrete specific detail. Pattison is the most systematic about the sensory-grounding piece; Davis is the most varied in her toolkit.

**Paul:** how about bookwriting?

**Claude:** Same basic territory — bypass the inner critic, generate raw material, organize later. Here are the main teachers:

**Natalie Goldberg** (_Writing Down the Bones_) — closest to Pattison in spirit. Timed "writing practice": pick a prompt, keep your hand moving, don't cross out, go for the jugular. The hand bypasses the censoring mind. Very Zen-influenced; she studied with a Zen teacher and treats writing practice like meditation.

**Julia Cameron** (_The Artist's Way_) — Morning Pages: three longhand pages every morning, stream of consciousness, no re-reading. Not meant to be good writing — meant to drain the "brain sludge" so the real creative work can flow. Hugely influential across all creative fields.

**Peter Elbow** (_Writing Without Teachers_) — coined "freewriting" as a formal technique. Timed, uncensored, no stopping. Also teaches "cooking" — letting ideas sit and ferment before shaping them. Distinguishes the generating self from the editing self and insists they can't operate simultaneously.

**Anne Lamott** (_Bird by Bird_) — "shitty first drafts." Permission to write badly is the whole game. Also teaches extreme narrowing of focus: don't write a book, write what you can see through a one-inch picture frame.

**Stephen King** (_On Writing_) — situational rather than plot-driven brainstorming. Put characters in a situation and watch what happens; don't outline, just discover. Keeps a notebook but trusts his subconscious more than any system.

**Dorothea Brande** (_Becoming a Writer_, 1934) — arguably the original. Write immediately upon waking before the rational mind engages. Predates Cameron by fifty years with essentially the same insight.

The through-line across all of them is identical to the songwriting teachers: separate generation from evaluation, use time pressure or physical momentum to outrun the critic, and treat the first draft as raw material not finished product. Goldberg and Pattison are almost the same pedagogy applied to different crafts.
