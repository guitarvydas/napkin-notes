So a complete, working Pong clone is under 500 lines of game logic (under 900 with its bundled helper libraries), while a full, production-grade Lua language implementation — lexer, parser, bytecode compiler, VM, GC, and standard libraries — comes in around 29,500 lines. That's roughly a 60:1 ratio, which tracks with the rest of your size comparisons: small, focused systems built on a tight core stay small even as functionality grows, since most of the bulk lives in the substrate, not the application.

*rough transistor count within chips used for Atari Pong 1972 vs. cs50 computer*

Both come from solid sourcing, with one bit of honest estimation in the middle:

**Atari Pong arcade board (1972)** — well documented: 66 TTL ICs (IEEE Spectrum, arcade-history.com, and Hugo Holden's circuit analysis all agree on this number), plus a pair of 555 timer chips and a handful of discrete transistors. No CPU, no software — pure hardwired logic.

There's no published aggregate transistor count for the board, so this part is an estimate: 7400-series SSI/MSI chips of that era typically run from ~16 transistors (a simple quad-NAND gate package) up to ~100+ (counters, shift registers), with flip-flops in between; a 555 timer has about 25 transistors internally. Averaging the mix of gates, flip-flops, and counters Pong actually used puts the board at roughly **2,000–3,000 transistors total**.

**M2 Pro (the chip in your Mac mini)** — Apple states this directly: **40 billion transistors**, on TSMC's 5nm-class process.

|Layer|Count|
|---|---|
|Pong arcade board (1972), ~66 TTL chips|~2,000–3,000 transistors (estimated)|
|M2 Pro SoC (your Mac mini)|40,000,000,000 transistors|

That's roughly a **16-million-fold** increase in transistor count between the machine Pong originally ran on and the chip now running a software clone of it inside a game engine inside a Lua VM. For reference against your own MC6800/6502 numbers (~3,500–4,100 transistors each), Pong's whole hardwired board was already in the same ballpark as one of those early 8-bit CPUs — it's the jump from "discrete TTL logic" to "single SoC" that does almost all the multiplying, not the difference between Pong's board and a first-generation microprocessor.
