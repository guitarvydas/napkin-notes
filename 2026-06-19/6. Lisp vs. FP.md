Anything with mutable data structures is not purely functional. Lisp 1.5 showed how to build a pure FP engine on top of a CPU. Sector Lisp does it even better (no heaps, pure FP garbage collection, unlike Lisp 1.5).

Pure FP is very restricted, but interesting. But, we seem to be unable to just leave it alone. I currently believe that severe bloat is caused by forcing non-FP concepts into the FP paradigm. Lisp, as we know it, is not pure FP. It is a kitchen sink full of anti-FP concepts, like mutability. Sector Lisp - pure FP - is extremely small (436 bytes). Instead of leaving the FP paradigm alone, we've been shoving anti-FP concepts into Lisp (and many other PLs) to "generalize" it. 

aside: Programming languages are just caveman IDEs for program development. The idea of shoving everything into a language is a very 1960s way of dealing with IDEs. Generalization seemed like a good idea in the 1960s, but we can do better today (e.g. using specialization and many little languages (the dislike for multiple languages is based on the kitchen-sink nature of current PLs)).
